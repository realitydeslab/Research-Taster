# Cultural Dimensions of Knowledge Production and Research Style

Literature review for the Research Taster project. Covers cultural influences on how research is conducted, valued, and disseminated across geographies and intellectual traditions.

---

## 1. Foundational Frameworks: Culture and Cognition

### 1.1 Nisbett, R. E. (2003). *The Geography of Thought: How Asians and Westerners Think Differently… and Why*. Free Press.
**Key contribution:** Landmark argument that East Asians tend toward holistic, context-dependent reasoning while Westerners favor analytic, object-focused cognition. Draws on experimental psychology to show these are not just philosophical preferences but deeply embedded cognitive styles.
**Relevance:** Foundational for understanding why research styles differ—if cognition itself varies culturally, so will hypothesis formation, methodology preference, and what counts as a good explanation.

### 1.2 Nisbett, R. E., Peng, K., Choi, I., & Norenzayan, A. (2001). Culture and systems of thought: Holistic versus analytic cognition. *Psychological Review*, 108(2), 291–310. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.108.2.291
**Key contribution:** Systematic review showing East Asians attend to context and relationships (holistic thought) while Westerners attend to objects and categories (analytic thought). Traces roots to ancient Greek vs. Chinese philosophical traditions.
**Relevance:** Directly explains why Eastern research traditions may favor systems-level, integrative approaches vs. Western reductionism.

### 1.3 Henrich, J., Heine, S. J., & Norenzayan, A. (2010). The weirdest people in the world? *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 33(2-3), 61–83. https://doi.org/10.1017/S0140525X0999152X
**Key contribution:** Demonstrated that most behavioral science findings are based on WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) populations, which are statistical outliers on many psychological dimensions. Called for broadening the empirical base.
**Relevance:** Core critique for Research Taster—reveals the systematic cultural bias in what counts as "established science." Any tool that surfaces research must grapple with this sampling bias.

### 1.4 Hofstede, G. (1980). *Culture's Consequences: International Differences in Work-Related Values*. Sage.
**Key contribution:** Introduced cultural dimensions (power distance, individualism/collectivism, uncertainty avoidance, masculinity/femininity) based on IBM employee surveys across 40+ countries. Later expanded to 6 dimensions.
**Relevance:** Hofstede's dimensions predict differences in academic hierarchy, risk-taking in research, and collaboration patterns. High power-distance cultures may produce more top-down, PI-driven research; individualist cultures reward novelty-seeking.

### 1.5 Hofstede, G. (1983). The cultural relativity of organizational practices and theories. *Journal of International Business Studies*, 14(2), 75–89. https://doi.org/10.1057/palgrave.jibs.8490867
**Key contribution:** Extended the cultural dimensions framework to argue that management theories (and by extension, research organization) are culturally specific, not universal.
**Relevance:** Implies that how labs are organized, how PhD students are supervised, and how research agendas are set all reflect cultural values.

## 2. East–West Research Styles and Academic Traditions

### 2.1 Needham, J. (1954–2004). *Science and Civilisation in China* (7 volumes). Cambridge University Press.
**Key contribution:** Monumental historical study documenting China's vast scientific and technological achievements, and asking why modern science emerged in Europe rather than China (the "Needham Question").
**Relevance:** Essential context for understanding Chinese research traditions—not as "catching up" to the West but as a parallel, sometimes earlier, knowledge system with different organizing principles.

### 2.2 Lloyd, G. E. R. (1996). *Adversaries and Authorities: Investigations into Ancient Greek and Chinese Science*. Cambridge University Press.
**Key contribution:** Compared ancient Greek and Chinese approaches to natural inquiry. Greeks favored adversarial debate and axiomatic proof; Chinese favored consensual, analogical reasoning aligned with statecraft.
**Relevance:** Shows that the divergence in research styles has millennia-deep roots in different social structures of knowledge validation.

### 2.3 Lloyd, G. E. R. (2004). *Ancient Worlds, Modern Reflections: Philosophical Perspectives on Greek and Chinese Science and Culture*. Oxford University Press.
**Key contribution:** Extended comparative analysis, arguing neither tradition is "superior"—they represent different but equally sophisticated responses to the challenge of understanding nature.
**Relevance:** Supports the Research Taster premise that multiple valid approaches to knowledge exist.

### 2.4 Kim, K.-M. (2017). Rethinking the distinction between Eastern and Western science. *East Asian Science, Technology and Society*, 11(2), 159–182. https://doi.org/10.1215/18752160-3823261
**Key contribution:** Critiques oversimplified East/West dichotomies in science studies, arguing for more nuanced understanding of how local contexts shape scientific practice without essentializing "Eastern" or "Western" approaches.
**Relevance:** Important corrective—Research Taster should avoid crude cultural stereotyping while still recognizing genuine differences.

### 2.5 Chua, H. F., Boland, J. E., & Nisbett, R. E. (2005). Cultural variation in eye movements during scene perception. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 102(35), 12629–12633. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0506162102
**Key contribution:** Showed Americans fixate more on focal objects while Chinese participants attend more to background/context, providing physiological evidence for cultural differences in perception.
**Relevance:** Suggests that even data observation and pattern recognition in research may be culturally inflected.

### 2.6 Masuda, T., & Nisbett, R. E. (2006). Culture and change blindness. *Cognitive Science*, 30(2), 381–399. https://doi.org/10.1207/s15516709cog0000_63
**Key contribution:** East Asians were more sensitive to changes in background context; Americans more to focal object changes. Demonstrates culture shapes literal perception.
**Relevance:** Extends the implication that what researchers notice, and therefore study, is shaped by cultural cognitive style.

## 3. National Innovation Systems and Research Cultures

### 3.1 Lundvall, B.-Å. (Ed.) (1992). *National Systems of Innovation: Towards a Theory of Innovation and Interactive Learning*. Pinter.
**Key contribution:** Foundational text arguing that innovation is shaped by national institutional frameworks—education, finance, regulation, labor markets—not just individual genius.
**Relevance:** Provides the macro-framework for understanding why countries produce different kinds of research.

### 3.2 Nelson, R. R. (Ed.) (1993). *National Innovation Systems: A Comparative Analysis*. Oxford University Press.
**Key contribution:** Comparative case studies of innovation systems in the US, Japan, Germany, and others, showing that institutional architecture shapes what gets researched and how.
**Relevance:** Empirical evidence that research style is nationally inflected.

### 3.3 Freeman, C. (1987). *Technology Policy and Economic Performance: Lessons from Japan*. Pinter.
**Key contribution:** Showed how Japan's distinctive innovation system (incremental improvement, close university-industry ties, consensus decision-making) produced a different style of technological advance than the US model.
**Relevance:** Demonstrates that "innovation" itself takes culturally specific forms—not just invention, but kaizen-style continuous improvement.

### 3.4 Shane, S. (1993). Cultural influences on national rates of innovation. *Journal of Business Venturing*, 8(1), 59–73. https://doi.org/10.1016/0883-9026(93)90011-S
**Key contribution:** Found that individualism (Hofstede dimension) positively predicted national innovation rates, while power distance negatively predicted them. First direct empirical link between cultural dimensions and innovation output.
**Relevance:** Quantitative evidence that cultural values shape research productivity and type.

### 3.5 Taylor, M. Z., & Wilson, S. (2012). Does culture still matter? The effects of individualism on national innovation rates. *Journal of Business Venturing*, 27(2), 234–247. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jbusvent.2010.10.001
**Key contribution:** Updated Shane's analysis with newer data, confirming that individualism remains a significant predictor of innovation even after controlling for institutional quality, GDP, and human capital.
**Relevance:** Reinforces that cultural values have an independent, persistent effect on research/innovation patterns.

## 4. Decolonizing Research and Indigenous Knowledge Systems

### 4.1 Smith, L. T. (1999/2012). *Decolonizing Methodologies: Research and Indigenous Peoples* (2nd ed.). Zed Books.
**Key contribution:** Seminal critique of Western research as a tool of colonialism. Argues for research methodologies grounded in Indigenous worldviews, emphasizing relationality, reciprocity, and community benefit.
**Relevance:** Fundamental challenge to the universality of Western research methods. Research Taster must consider whose knowledge counts.

### 4.2 Chilisa, B. (2012). *Indigenous Research Methodologies*. Sage.
**Key contribution:** Provides practical frameworks for conducting research from African and other non-Western epistemological standpoints, including relational ontologies and communal knowledge validation.
**Relevance:** Offers alternative research paradigms that Research Taster could help surface and legitimize.

### 4.3 Chilisa, B., & Tsheko, G. N. (2014). Mixed methods in indigenous research: Building relationships for sustainable intervention outcomes. *Journal of Mixed Methods Research*, 8(3), 222–233. https://doi.org/10.1177/1558689814527878
**Key contribution:** Demonstrates how mixed methods can be adapted to indigenous research contexts by centering relational accountability and community participation.
**Relevance:** Shows how mainstream methods can be culturally adapted rather than simply imposed.

### 4.4 Kovach, M. (2009). *Indigenous Methodologies: Characteristics, Conversations, and Contexts*. University of Toronto Press.
**Key contribution:** Articulates Indigenous research methodologies rooted in oral tradition, story, and holistic worldviews, primarily from Canadian First Nations perspectives.
**Relevance:** Another complete alternative epistemological framework for research practice.

### 4.5 Wilson, S. (2008). *Research Is Ceremony: Indigenous Research Methods*. Fernwood Publishing.
**Key contribution:** Frames research as relational ceremony, arguing that Indigenous epistemology requires fundamentally different ethical and methodological commitments than Western paradigms.
**Relevance:** Challenges the assumption that "good research" looks the same everywhere.

### 4.6 Agrawal, A. (1995). Dismantling the divide between indigenous and scientific knowledge. *Development and Change*, 26(3), 413–439. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-7660.1995.tb00560.x
**Key contribution:** Critiqued the rigid separation of "indigenous" from "scientific" knowledge, arguing they share features and that the divide serves political rather than epistemological purposes.
**Relevance:** Complicates simplistic categorization of knowledge types—important for Research Taster's taxonomic decisions.

### 4.7 Harding, S. (Ed.) (2011). *The Postcolonial Science and Technology Studies Reader*. Duke University Press.
**Key contribution:** Collected essays on how colonialism shaped what counts as science, whose knowledge is valued, and how postcolonial perspectives can enrich science studies.
**Relevance:** Broad theoretical grounding for why geography and power matter in knowledge production.

## 5. Cultural Epistemology and Philosophy of Science

### 5.1 Kuhn, T. S. (1962). *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Introduced paradigm theory—the idea that science operates within culturally and historically situated frameworks that determine what questions are asked and what counts as evidence.
**Relevance:** Though not explicitly about cross-cultural differences, provides the theoretical basis for understanding science as culturally embedded.

### 5.2 Feyerabend, P. (1975). *Against Method: Outline of an Anarchistic Theory of Knowledge*. New Left Books.
**Key contribution:** Argued against any single scientific method, advocating epistemological pluralism ("anything goes"). Suggested that non-Western knowledge traditions have been suppressed by methodological imperialism.
**Relevance:** Strongest philosophical argument for the legitimacy of diverse research styles.

### 5.3 Longino, H. E. (1990). *Science as Social Knowledge: Values and Objectivity in Scientific Inquiry*. Princeton University Press.
**Key contribution:** Showed that scientific knowledge is inherently social—shaped by community values, background assumptions, and social structures. Objectivity comes from critical discourse, not individual method.
**Relevance:** If science is social, then different societies will produce different (but potentially equally valid) science.

### 5.4 Harding, S. (1998). *Is Science Multicultural? Postcolonialisms, Feminisms, and Epistemologies*. Indiana University Press.
**Key contribution:** Argued for "strong objectivity" that includes standpoint epistemology—recognizing that the social position of researchers shapes what they can know. Called for genuinely multicultural sciences.
**Relevance:** Direct argument that Research Taster should surface diverse standpoints, not just dominant ones.

### 5.5 Turnbull, D. (2000). *Masons, Tricksters and Cartographers: Comparative Studies in the Sociology of Scientific and Indigenous Knowledge*. Routledge.
**Key contribution:** Compared Western scientific knowledge systems (cathedral building, cartography) with indigenous and non-Western ones, showing all are "local" knowledge assemblages that get universalized through social processes.
**Relevance:** Undermines the idea that Western science is uniquely universal—all knowledge is produced locally and then travels.

### 5.6 Santos, B. de S. (2014). *Epistemologies of the South: Justice Against Epistemicide*. Paradigm Publishers.
**Key contribution:** Coined "epistemicide"—the destruction of non-Western knowledge systems by colonial modernity. Called for an "ecology of knowledges" that recognizes the validity of diverse epistemic traditions.
**Relevance:** Powerful framing for why knowledge diversity matters. Research Taster could help reverse epistemicide by making non-Western research visible.

## 6. Cross-Cultural Scientometrics and Publication Patterns

### 6.1 Leydesdorff, L., & Wagner, C. S. (2008). International collaboration in science and the formation of a core group. *Journal of Informetrics*, 2(4), 317–325. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.joi.2008.07.003
**Key contribution:** Showed that international scientific collaboration is structured around a core of wealthy nations, with peripheral countries less integrated. Collaboration patterns reflect geopolitical power.
**Relevance:** Demonstrates that the global research landscape is not neutral—it encodes power asymmetries.

### 6.2 Marginson, S. (2022). *Higher Education in the Global Knowledge Economy*. In *Space and Scale in Higher Education*. Cambridge University Press.
**Key contribution:** Analyzed how globalization of higher education creates both convergence (toward Anglo-American norms) and divergence (national traditions persisting), with Confucian-heritage systems as a key case.
**Relevance:** Shows how research norms are simultaneously globalizing and remaining culturally specific.

### 6.3 Núñez, M. A., Barlow, J., Cadotte, M., et al. (2019). Assessing the uneven global distribution of readership, submissions and publications in applied ecology. *Journal of Applied Ecology*, 56(1), 4–9. https://doi.org/10.1111/1365-2664.13294
**Key contribution:** Documented extreme geographic bias in ecological publishing—Global South researchers underrepresented as authors, reviewers, and editors despite studying the most biodiverse regions.
**Relevance:** Concrete evidence of how knowledge production geography creates blind spots.

### 6.4 Meho, L. I. (2007). The rise and rise of citation analysis. *Physics World*, 20(1), 32–36.
**Key contribution:** Overview of how citation-based metrics became dominant in evaluating research, with discussion of how this system disadvantages non-English, non-Western research traditions.
**Relevance:** The metrics by which Research Taster evaluates papers may themselves be culturally biased.

### 6.5 Rafols, I., Leydesdorff, L., O'Hare, A., Nightingale, P., & Stirling, A. (2012). How journal rankings can suppress interdisciplinary research. *Research Policy*, 41(7), 1262–1282. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.respol.2012.03.015
**Key contribution:** Showed that journal ranking systems systematically disadvantage interdisciplinary research, which is more common in some cultural traditions (e.g., Chinese systems thinking).
**Relevance:** Research Taster's ranking/filtering methods could inadvertently replicate this bias.

## 7. Culture, Collaboration, and Research Practice

### 7.1 Traweek, S. (1988). *Beamtimes and Lifetimes: The World of High Energy Physicists*. Harvard University Press.
**Key contribution:** Ethnographic comparison of US and Japanese physics labs, revealing profoundly different social organization, aesthetic values, career structures, and relationships to theory/experiment.
**Relevance:** One of the best empirical studies of how the same discipline is practiced differently across cultures.

### 7.2 Knorr Cetina, K. (1999). *Epistemic Cultures: How the Sciences Make Knowledge*. Harvard University Press.
**Key contribution:** Compared high-energy physics and molecular biology, showing that even within Western science, different fields have fundamentally different "epistemic cultures"—ways of creating and validating knowledge.
**Relevance:** If epistemic cultures differ within one country's science, cross-national differences are even more profound.

### 7.3 Merton, R. K. (1973). *The Sociology of Science: Theoretical and Empirical Investigations*. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Defined the norms of Western science (universalism, communism, disinterestedness, organized skepticism—CUDOS). These norms are often treated as universal but are culturally specific to the Western Enlightenment tradition.
**Relevance:** Merton's norms are the implicit standard by which much research is evaluated—understanding their cultural specificity is essential.

### 7.4 Hwang, K.-K. (2005). The third wave of cultural psychology: The indigenous movement. *The Psychologist*, 18(2), 80–83.
**Key contribution:** Argued for "indigenous psychologies" that develop theories from within cultural contexts rather than importing Western frameworks. Part of a broader movement in Asian social science.
**Relevance:** Parallel to Research Taster's challenge—how to value locally-grounded knowledge in a globally connected system.

### 7.5 Alatas, S. F. (2003). Academic dependency and the global division of labour in the social sciences. *Current Sociology*, 51(6), 599–613. https://doi.org/10.1177/00113921030516003
**Key contribution:** Analyzed how social science in the Global South is dependent on Western theory, methods, and publication outlets, creating a "captive mind" that reproduces rather than challenges dominant frameworks.
**Relevance:** Critical diagnosis of the structural problem Research Taster might help address.

## 8. Language, Publication, and Knowledge Geopolitics

### 8.1 Gordin, M. D. (2015). *Scientific Babel: How Science Was Done Before and After Global English*. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Historical account of how science went from being multilingual (Latin, French, German, Russian, Japanese) to English-dominated. Shows that language shifts reshape what knowledge is accessible and valued.
**Relevance:** Research Taster operates in an English-dominated landscape—non-English research is systematically invisible.

### 8.2 Canagarajah, A. S. (2002). *A Geopolitics of Academic Writing*. University of Pittsburgh Press.
**Key contribution:** Showed how academic writing conventions are not neutral but reflect Anglo-American rhetorical norms that disadvantage scholars from other traditions. Publishing success requires cultural conformity.
**Relevance:** Even when non-Western scholars publish, they must adopt Western rhetorical styles, potentially losing culturally distinctive insights.

### 8.3 Salager-Meyer, F. (2008). Scientific publishing in developing countries: Challenges for the future. *Journal of English for Academic Purposes*, 7(2), 121–132. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jeap.2008.03.009
**Key contribution:** Documented the structural barriers to scientific publishing from developing countries: language, infrastructure, mentorship, and journal bias.
**Relevance:** Helps explain why Research Taster's corpus may under-represent Global South knowledge.

### 8.4 Lillis, T., & Curry, M. J. (2010). *Academic Writing in a Global Context: The Politics and Practices of Publishing in English*. Routledge.
**Key contribution:** Based on longitudinal study of multilingual scholars, documented how the pressure to publish in English reshapes research agendas and devalues local knowledge.
**Relevance:** Shows the mechanism by which language hegemony shapes the global research landscape.

## 9. Comparative Innovation and Creativity Across Cultures

### 9.1 Niu, W., & Sternberg, R. J. (2001). Cultural influences on artistic creativity and its evaluation. *International Journal of Psychology*, 36(4), 225–241. https://doi.org/10.1080/00207590143000036
**Key contribution:** Showed that Eastern and Western conceptions of creativity differ fundamentally—Westerners emphasize novelty while Chinese conceptions include moral value, social contribution, and connection to tradition.
**Relevance:** If "creativity" means different things, then "innovative research" will be evaluated differently across cultures.

### 9.2 Morris, M. W., & Leung, K. (2010). Creativity East and West: Perspectives and parallels. *Management and Organization Review*, 6(3), 313–327. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1740-8784.2010.00193.x
**Key contribution:** Reviewed cross-cultural creativity research, finding that while individualism may foster some kinds of novelty, collectivist cultures excel at different forms of creative problem-solving (e.g., incremental innovation, synthesis).
**Relevance:** Challenges the assumption that the most "original" research (by Western standards) is the most valuable.

### 9.3 Lubart, T. I. (1999). Creativity across cultures. In R. J. Sternberg (Ed.), *Handbook of Creativity* (pp. 339–350). Cambridge University Press.
**Key contribution:** Comprehensive review of how culture shapes the definition, expression, and assessment of creativity. Shows creativity is not a culture-free concept.
**Relevance:** Underscores that Research Taster's evaluation criteria for "interesting" or "breakthrough" research are culturally loaded.

## 10. Systems Thinking and Holistic Approaches

### 10.1 Capra, F. (1975). *The Tao of Physics: An Exploration of the Parallels Between Modern Physics and Eastern Mysticism*. Shambhala.
**Key contribution:** Drew parallels between quantum physics and Eastern philosophical traditions (Buddhism, Hinduism, Taoism), arguing that modern physics was converging with holistic Eastern worldviews.
**Relevance:** Early popular argument that Eastern epistemologies offer insights complementary to Western science.

### 10.2 Zhu, Z. (1999). Systems approaches: Where the East meets the West? *World Futures: Journal of General Evolution*, 53(4), 283–303. https://doi.org/10.1080/02604027.1999.9972742
**Key contribution:** Argued that systems thinking represents a convergence point between Eastern holistic philosophy and Western analytical traditions, but that each tradition brings different strengths to systems methodology.
**Relevance:** Suggests Research Taster could identify synthesis opportunities where different traditions complement each other.

### 10.3 Nonaka, I., & Takeuchi, H. (1995). *The Knowledge-Creating Company: How Japanese Companies Create the Dynamics of Innovation*. Oxford University Press.
**Key contribution:** Described how Japanese companies create knowledge through a cycle of tacit-explicit knowledge conversion (SECI model), emphasizing tacit knowledge and socialization—reflecting Japanese cultural values.
**Relevance:** Shows how cultural values shape not just what is known but how knowledge itself is created and shared.

## Key Themes for Research Taster

1. **Cognitive style shapes research style:** Holistic vs. analytic cognition (Nisbett) maps onto integrative vs. reductionist research approaches
2. **WEIRD sampling bias:** Most "universal" findings are culturally specific (Henrich et al.)—Research Taster must account for this
3. **Epistemic pluralism:** Multiple valid ways of knowing exist (Santos, Feyerabend, Harding)—a tool that only surfaces Western research perpetuates epistemicide
4. **Language hegemony:** English dominance makes non-English research invisible (Gordin, Canagarajah)
5. **Metrics bias:** Citation-based evaluation disadvantages non-Western, interdisciplinary, and Global South research (Rafols et al., Núñez et al.)
6. **Innovation takes many forms:** Novelty-seeking is one cultural model; synthesis, refinement, and contextual adaptation are others (Niu & Sternberg, Freeman)
7. **Institutional architecture matters:** National innovation systems shape what research gets done (Lundvall, Nelson)
8. **Knowledge is always local before it's universal:** All knowledge systems are situated; universality is achieved through social processes, not inherent properties (Turnbull)
