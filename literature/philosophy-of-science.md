# Philosophy of Science: Research Taste, Aesthetic Judgment, and Epistemic Values

Literature review for the Research Taster project.

---

## 1. Research Taste — Direct Treatments

### 1.1 Koltun, V., & Hafner, D. (2021). A Measure of Research Taste. *arXiv preprint arXiv:2105.08089*.
- **Key contribution:** Proposes CAP, a citation-based metric that rewards both productivity and taste — the researcher's ability to focus on impactful contributions rather than maximizing publication count.
- **Relevance:** The most direct operationalization of "research taste" as a measurable construct. Core reference for the Research Taster project.

### 1.2 Chiu, D. M. (2009). On Research Taste. Unpublished manuscript, CUHK.
- **Key contribution:** Reflective essay on how a researcher's choices — problem selection, methodology, style — constitute their "research taste" and shape their career trajectory.
- **Relevance:** Early explicit use of "research taste" as a concept in CS research mentorship.

### 1.3 Fernandez, R. C. (2018). Aurum: A Story About Research Taste. In *Making Databases Work: The Pragmatic Wisdom of Michael Stonebraker*, ACM.
- **Key contribution:** Narrative account of how research taste manifests in systems research — choosing problems that matter to practitioners vs. academic novelty.
- **Relevance:** Case study of taste in applied CS research; shows taste as practical judgment about problem significance.

### 1.4 Hermanowicz, J. C. (2006). What Does It Take to Be Successful? *Science, Technology, & Human Values*, 31(2), 135–152.
- **Key contribution:** Interview study of physicists revealing that problem selection, follow-through, and "taste" in choosing productive research directions are key success factors beyond raw talent.
- **Relevance:** Empirical evidence that scientists themselves identify taste/judgment in problem choice as critical to success.

### 1.5 Wang, H., Wu, Y., & Xu, H. (2025). The Publication Choice Problem. *arXiv preprint arXiv:2511.13678*.
- **Key contribution:** Game-theoretic model where researcher "type" captures productivity and research taste, analyzing how publication incentives shape research behavior.
- **Relevance:** Formal modeling of how taste interacts with institutional incentives.

## 2. Aesthetic Judgment in Science

### 2.1 McAllister, J. W. (1996). *Beauty and Revolution in Science*. Cornell University Press.
- **Key contribution:** Seminal book arguing that scientists hold aesthetic criteria for theory evaluation, and that scientific revolutions involve aesthetic ruptures — shifts in what properties are considered beautiful.
- **Relevance:** Foundational framework for understanding how aesthetic preferences function as quasi-epistemic criteria in science.

### 2.2 McAllister, J. W. (1989). Truth and Beauty in Scientific Reason. *Synthese*, 78(1), 25–51.
- **Key contribution:** Argues aesthetic criteria in science are inductively grounded — scientists learn to find beautiful those properties historically correlated with empirical success.
- **Relevance:** Provides a rationalist account of how "taste" in theory choice can be epistemically justified.

### 2.3 McAllister, J. W. (1990). Dirac and the Aesthetic Evaluation of Theories. *Methodology and Science*, 23, 174–191.
- **Key contribution:** Case study of Dirac's explicit use of mathematical beauty as a guide to theory construction in physics.
- **Relevance:** Historical example of aesthetic judgment functioning as research taste in physics.

### 2.4 McAllister, J. W. (1991). Scientists' Aesthetic Judgements. *British Journal of Aesthetics*, 31(4), 332–341.
- **Key contribution:** Analyzes what it means for a scientist to form an aesthetic judgment on a theory, distinguishing aesthetic from empirical evaluation.
- **Relevance:** Philosophical clarification of aesthetic judgment as distinct from but related to epistemic evaluation.

### 2.5 Ivanova, M. (2017). Aesthetic Values in Science. *Philosophy Compass*, 12(10), e12433.
- **Key contribution:** Comprehensive survey of how aesthetic considerations influence scientific reasoning, covering McAllister, Kuhn, and recent debates on whether beauty tracks truth.
- **Relevance:** Excellent overview article connecting aesthetics to epistemic values in science.

### 2.6 Davies, D. (1998). McAllister's Aesthetics in Science: A Critical Notice. *International Studies in the Philosophy of Science*, 12(1), 53–62.
- **Key contribution:** Critical evaluation of McAllister's rationalist account of aesthetics in science, raising questions about the relationship between aesthetic criteria and Kuhnian normal science.
- **Relevance:** Important counterpoint to naive accounts of beauty guiding science.

### 2.7 Chandrasekhar, S. (1987). *Truth and Beauty: Aesthetics and Motivations in Science*. University of Chicago Press.
- **Key contribution:** Nobel laureate's reflections on the role of beauty and elegance as motivating forces in physics and astrophysics.
- **Relevance:** First-person account of aesthetic judgment as research motivation from a practicing scientist.

### 2.8 Glynn, I. (2013). *Elegance in Science: The Beauty of Simplicity*. Oxford University Press.
- **Key contribution:** Explores how the pursuit of elegance and simplicity has guided major scientific discoveries across disciplines.
- **Relevance:** Shows elegance as a cross-disciplinary aesthetic criterion that functions like taste.

### 2.9 Rota, G.-C. (1997). The Phenomenology of Mathematical Beauty. *Synthese*, 111(2), 171–182.
- **Key contribution:** Distinguished mathematician's philosophical analysis of what mathematical beauty actually is, arguing it is distinct from elegance and is not merely subjective.
- **Relevance:** Rigorous phenomenological account of aesthetic judgment in mathematics — a core case for "taste" in formal sciences.

### 2.10 Tsilikis, J. D. (1959). Simplicity and Elegance in Theoretical Physics. *American Scientist*, 47(1), 87–96.
- **Key contribution:** Early discussion of how elegance assists in selecting physical content and formulating mathematical structure of theories.
- **Relevance:** Historical evidence that aesthetic criteria have long been explicitly discussed in physics.

## 3. Kuhn's Values and Theory Choice

### 3.1 Kuhn, T. S. (1977). Objectivity, Value Judgment, and Theory Choice. In *The Essential Tension*, 320–339. University of Chicago Press.
- **Key contribution:** Identifies five values guiding theory choice — accuracy, consistency, scope, simplicity, and fruitfulness — arguing they function as values (requiring judgment) rather than rules (mechanically applied).
- **Relevance:** The foundational text on epistemic values in science. Kuhn's values are essentially a formal articulation of scientific "taste."

### 3.2 Sankey, H. (2021). Realism and the Epistemic Objectivity of Science. *Kriterion — Journal of Philosophy*, 35(1), 1–22.
- **Key contribution:** Explores the extent to which Kuhn's epistemic values provide an objective basis for theory choice and epistemic justification.
- **Relevance:** Connects Kuhn's values framework to questions of objectivity — whether taste can be "correct."

### 3.3 Hempel, C. G. (1983). Valuation and Objectivity in Science. In *A Portrait of Twenty-five Years: Boston Colloquium for the Philosophy of Science*, 73–100. Springer.
- **Key contribution:** Examines Kuhn's claim that science is the best example of rationality and analyzes the role of value judgments in scientific methodology.
- **Relevance:** Early philosophical response to Kuhn's values framework with implications for understanding taste as rational.

### 3.4 Pirozelli, P. (2019). Thomas Kuhn's Theory of Rationality. *Manuscrito*, 42(3), 1–46.
- **Key contribution:** Reconstructs Kuhn's implicit theory of rational choice in science, showing how epistemic values allow for rational disagreement.
- **Relevance:** Shows how taste-based disagreements in science can still be rational.

## 4. Theoretical Virtues

### 4.1 Keas, M. N. (2018). Systematizing the Theoretical Virtues. *Synthese*, 195(6), 2761–2793.
- **Key contribution:** Comprehensive taxonomy of theoretical virtues — evidential (empirical fit), coherential (consistency), aesthetic (beauty, simplicity, elegance), and diachronic (durability, fruitfulness).
- **Relevance:** Maps the full space of criteria that constitute "good taste" in theory evaluation.

### 4.2 McMullin, E. (2008). The Virtues of a Good Theory. In *The Routledge Companion to Philosophy of Science*, 498–508.
- **Key contribution:** Classic analysis of what makes a theory "good" — including fertility, explanatory power, and simplicity as internal virtues guiding theory acceptance.
- **Relevance:** Articulates the normative criteria that underlie research taste in theory selection.

### 4.3 Baker, A. (2004/2022). Simplicity. *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*.
- **Key contribution:** Comprehensive survey of simplicity as a theoretical virtue, examining why scientists prefer simpler theories and whether this preference is epistemically justified.
- **Relevance:** Deep dive into one key component of scientific taste.

### 4.4 Lichacz, P. (2021). Epistemic Simplicity — A Virtue or a Vice? *Metaphilosophy*, 52(6), 686–705.
- **Key contribution:** Challenges whether simplicity is genuinely an epistemic virtue, arguing its status is more ambiguous than usually assumed.
- **Relevance:** Critical perspective on whether common taste heuristics are epistemically reliable.

### 4.5 Pence, C. H. (2025). Virtues and Pursuit-Worthiness. In *Integrative Promise: Explanatory Virtues in Biology*. Springer.
- **Key contribution:** Distinguishes theoretical from explanatory virtues and argues for fruitfulness as a key criterion for what research is "pursuit-worthy."
- **Relevance:** Directly connects virtue framework to the question of which research to pursue — i.e., taste.

## 5. Epistemic Virtues in Science

### 5.1 Stump, D. J. (2007). Virtues and Vices in Scientific Practice. *Synthese*, 149(3) [published online 2016].
- **Key contribution:** Argues intellectual virtues accelerate convergence in scientific inquiry, playing a role in theory choice that is neither necessary nor sufficient but practically important.
- **Relevance:** Connects virtue epistemology to actual scientific practice — virtues as the character traits underlying good taste.

### 5.2 Howes, M. (2015). Objectivity, Intellectual Virtue, and Community. In *Objectivity in Science: New Perspectives from Science and Technology Studies*, 173–190. Springer.
- **Key contribution:** Argues that epistemic virtues are socially shaped — communities cultivate cognitive character through epistemically virtuous or vicious relations.
- **Relevance:** Shows taste is not purely individual but shaped by scientific communities.

## 6. Tacit Knowledge and Personal Knowledge

### 6.1 Polanyi, M. (1958). *Personal Knowledge: Towards a Post-Critical Philosophy*. Routledge.
- **Key contribution:** Seminal work arguing that all knowledge, including scientific knowledge, has an irreducible personal component. Scientists rely on tacit knowledge — skills, intuitions, and judgments that cannot be fully articulated.
- **Relevance:** The philosophical foundation for understanding research taste as tacit knowledge — knowing what problems are worth pursuing without being able to fully articulate why.

### 6.2 Polanyi, M. (1966). The Logic of Tacit Inference. *Philosophy*, 41(155), 1–18.
- **Key contribution:** Formalizes the structure of tacit knowing — how subsidiary awareness of unspecifiable clues integrates into focal knowledge.
- **Relevance:** Provides the epistemological mechanism by which taste operates: pattern recognition below the threshold of explicit articulation.

### 6.3 Nye, M. J. (2017). Michael Polanyi: Science as Personal Knowledge and Social Practice. *Angewandte Chemie International Edition*, 56(9), 2214–2220.
- **Key contribution:** Contextualizes Polanyi's philosophy within the history of science, showing how personal knowledge operates in laboratory practice.
- **Relevance:** Connects Polanyi's framework to concrete scientific practice.

### 6.4 Jacobs, S. (2000). Michael Polanyi on the Education and Knowledge of Scientists. *Science & Education*, 9(3), 267–280.
- **Key contribution:** Analyzes Polanyi's conception of scientific research as an "art-form" requiring apprenticeship — discoveries are made through application of personal, tacit skills.
- **Relevance:** Taste as something learned through mentorship, not textbooks — key for understanding how to cultivate it.

### 6.5 Fischer, F., & Mandell, A. (2009). Michael Polanyi's Republic of Science: The Tacit Dimension. *Science as Culture*, 18(1), 23–46.
- **Key contribution:** Examines Polanyi's vision of the scientific community as self-governing through shared tacit standards, and how the tacit dimension shapes the process of scientific discovery.
- **Relevance:** Connects individual taste to community norms — taste as socially calibrated tacit knowledge.

## 7. Bourdieu and Scientific Habitus

### 7.1 Bourdieu, P. (1975). The Specificity of the Scientific Field and the Social Conditions of the Progress of Reason. *Social Science Information*, 14(6), 19–47.
- **Key contribution:** Foundational paper defining the scientific field as a competitive space where agents struggle for scientific capital, with habitus shaping dispositions toward certain research strategies.
- **Relevance:** The original sociological framework for understanding taste as habitus — internalized dispositions shaped by position in the scientific field.

### 7.2 Bourdieu, P. (2004). *Science of Science and Reflexivity*. University of Chicago Press.
- **Key contribution:** Bourdieu's mature synthesis applying his field theory to science itself, analyzing how scientific habitus, capital, and field structures shape what counts as legitimate research.
- **Relevance:** Most complete Bourdieusian account of how "taste" in science is socially structured.

### 7.3 Lenoir, R. (2006). Scientific Habitus: Pierre Bourdieu and the Collective Intellectual. *Theory, Culture & Society*, 23(6), 25–43.
- **Key contribution:** Analyzes Bourdieu's concept of scientific habitus within his broader theoretical system of habitus, field, and capital.
- **Relevance:** Clarifies what "scientific habitus" means and how it relates to individual and collective research dispositions.

### 7.4 Jeon, J. (2019). Rethinking Scientific Habitus: Toward a Theory of Embodiment, Institutions, and Stratification of Science. *Engaging Science, Technology, and Society*, 5, 160–179.
- **Key contribution:** Extends Bourdieu's scientific habitus concept to account for embodiment and institutional stratification, arguing the original formulation is underdeveloped.
- **Relevance:** Updates Bourdieu's framework for contemporary science — how taste is embodied and institutionally shaped.

### 7.5 Simon, R. M. (2011). Habitus and Utopia in Science: Bourdieu, Mannheim, and the Role of Specialties in the Scientific Field. *Studies in Sociology of Science*, 2(1), 29–41.
- **Key contribution:** Examines how scientific capital and habitus reflect the state of a science as it exists in a particular specialty, connecting Bourdieu to Mannheim's sociology of knowledge.
- **Relevance:** Shows how taste varies by specialty — different fields cultivate different habitus.

### 7.6 Mendoza, P., Kuntz, A. M., & Berger, J. B. (2012). Bourdieu and Academic Capitalism: Faculty "Habitus" in Materials Science and Engineering. *The Journal of Higher Education*, 83(4), 558–581.
- **Key contribution:** Empirical study of how faculty habitus in materials science is shaped by academic capitalism — market forces reshaping what constitutes valued research.
- **Relevance:** Shows how external forces (funding, industry) can reshape research taste.

### 7.7 Schirone, M. (2023). Field, Capital, and Habitus: The Impact of Pierre Bourdieu on Bibliometrics. *Quantitative Science Studies*, 4(1), 186–209.
- **Key contribution:** Maps the influence of Bourdieu's concepts on bibliometric research, showing how field, capital, and habitus have been operationalized in science studies.
- **Relevance:** Connects Bourdieu's framework to quantitative approaches — relevant for operationalizing taste.

## 8. Problem Choice in Science

### 8.1 Zuckerman, H. (1978). Theory Choice and Problem Choice in Science. *Sociological Inquiry*, 48(3–4), 65–95.
- **Key contribution:** Seminal sociological analysis of how scientists choose problems, examining the roles of cognitive factors, competition, and reward structures.
- **Relevance:** Directly addresses the central question of Research Taster — what drives problem choice.

### 8.2 Reskin, B. F. (1977). Scientific Productivity and the Reward Structure of Science. *American Sociological Review*, 42(3), 491–504.
- **Key contribution:** Shows that scientists must choose questions that are solvable yet nontrivial, and that collegial recognition structures shape these choices.
- **Relevance:** Links problem choice to social reward structures — taste as socially calibrated.

### 8.3 Cohen, B. A. (2017). How Should Novelty Be Valued in Science? *eLife*, 6, e28699.
- **Key contribution:** Argues that the scientific community over-values novelty at the expense of replication and incremental progress, questioning whether current taste norms serve science well.
- **Relevance:** Critical perspective on whether collective "taste" for novelty is epistemically well-calibrated.

### 8.4 Leahey, E., Lee, J., & Funk, R. J. (2023). What Types of Novelty Are Most Disruptive? *American Sociological Review*, 88(3), 562–597.
- **Key contribution:** Distinguishes types of novelty (new connections across domains vs. new within-domain contributions) and shows that cross-domain novelty is most disruptive.
- **Relevance:** Empirical basis for understanding what kind of "tasteful" problem choices lead to breakthrough research.

## 9. Beauty and Aesthetics in Mathematics and Physics

### 9.1 Langlands, R. P. (2010). Is There Beauty in Mathematical Theories? Unpublished lecture, University of Notre Dame.
- **Key contribution:** Reflections by a leading mathematician on whether and how beauty functions in mathematical theory, distinguishing deep beauty from surface elegance.
- **Relevance:** First-person account of aesthetic judgment in mathematics from one of its greatest practitioners.

## 10. Scientific Creativity and Intuition

### 10.1 Policastro, E. (1995). Creative Intuition: An Integrative Review. *Creativity Research Journal*, 8(2), 99–113.
- **Key contribution:** Reviews the role of intuition in creative processes across professions including science, identifying intuition as a form of tacit pattern recognition guiding creative production.
- **Relevance:** Connects intuition (a component of taste) to creativity in scientific work.

### 10.2 Burnett, C., & Francisco, J. (2020). Role of Intuition in Creativity. In *Encyclopedia of Creativity, Invention, Innovation and Entrepreneurship*, 2102–2107. Springer.
- **Key contribution:** Reviews how intuition operates in creative problem-solving, including scientific discovery as a process of regarding old problems from new perspectives.
- **Relevance:** Theoretical framework for the intuitive component of research taste.

## Summary Statistics

- **Total papers/books:** 38
- **Date range:** 1958–2025
- **Key themes:** Research taste as measurable construct (Koltun & Hafner), aesthetic judgment in theory choice (McAllister, Ivanova), Kuhn's epistemic values, theoretical virtues (Keas, McMullin), tacit knowledge (Polanyi), scientific habitus (Bourdieu), problem choice sociology (Zuckerman, Reskin), beauty in mathematics/physics (Rota, Chandrasekhar)
- **Core insight:** "Research taste" sits at the intersection of philosophy (epistemic values, aesthetic judgment), sociology (habitus, field, capital), and cognitive science (tacit knowledge, intuition). No single framework captures it fully — the Research Taster project should draw on all three traditions.
