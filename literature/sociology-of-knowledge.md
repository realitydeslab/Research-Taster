# Sociology of Knowledge and Epistemic Cultures

Literature review: How research communities develop shared taste, style, and epistemic norms.

---

## Foundational Frameworks

### 1. Fleck, L. (1935/1979). *Genesis and Development of a Scientific Fact*. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Introduced "thought style" (Denkstil) and "thought collective" (Denkkollektiv) — the idea that scientific facts are socially constructed within communities sharing cognitive norms, perceptual habits, and aesthetic sensibilities.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Direct precursor to the idea that research communities develop shared "taste." Fleck's thought style = the collective aesthetic/cognitive filter that determines what counts as interesting, elegant, or valid.

### 2. Kuhn, T.S. (1962/1970). *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Introduced "paradigm" and later "disciplinary matrix" — the shared commitments (symbolic generalizations, models, values, exemplars) that bind a scientific community. Normal science operates within paradigmatic consensus; revolutions replace it.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Paradigms encode implicit taste — what problems matter, what solutions look right. The exemplar concept (concrete problem-solutions learned by doing) is especially relevant to how taste is transmitted through apprenticeship.

### 3. Kuhn, T.S. (1977). "Objectivity, Value Judgment, and Theory Choice." In *The Essential Tension*, pp. 320–339. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Identified five values that guide theory choice (accuracy, consistency, scope, simplicity, fruitfulness) but argued scientists weight them differently — this weighting constitutes individual scientific "taste."
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Kuhn explicitly uses the word "taste" for the subjective element in scientific judgment. This is the locus classicus for the concept.

## Epistemic Cultures and Laboratory Studies

### 4. Knorr-Cetina, K. (1999). *Epistemic Cultures: How the Sciences Make Knowledge*. Harvard University Press.
**Key contribution:** Compared high-energy physics (CERN) and molecular biology to show that different fields have fundamentally different "machineries of knowledge construction" — different epistemic cultures with distinct practices, objects, and social arrangements. Cited 10,000+.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Core framework. Epistemic cultures = the deep structure of how communities produce knowledge, including implicit aesthetics, preferred methods, and what counts as convincing. Directly models how "taste" is institutionalized in practice.

### 5. Knorr-Cetina, K. (1981). *The Manufacture of Knowledge: An Essay on the Constructivist and Contextual Nature of Science*. Pergamon Press.
**Key contribution:** Pioneering laboratory ethnography showing that scientific decisions are shaped by local, contextual, and social factors — scientists constantly orient to the expected responses of specific community members. Cited 6,600+.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Shows taste as socially embedded — researchers calibrate their choices to anticipated peer reception, making community norms constitutive of knowledge production.

### 6. Knorr-Cetina, K. (2007). "Culture in Global Knowledge Societies: Knowledge Cultures and Epistemic Cultures." *Interdisciplinary Science Reviews*, 32(4), 361–375.
**Key contribution:** Extended epistemic cultures framework to global knowledge societies, showing how internalized practices and mechanisms shape knowledge creation across domains.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Bridges epistemic cultures to broader cultural analysis of knowledge-intensive communities.

### 7. Knorr-Cetina, K. (1991). "Epistemic Cultures: Forms of Reason in Science." *History of Political Economy*, 23(1), 105–122.
**Key contribution:** Early articulation of the epistemic cultures concept, foregrounding the "disunity" of science in regard to epistemic practice — different sciences reason differently.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Emphasizes that taste/style is not universal but discipline-specific.

### 8. Latour, B. & Woolgar, S. (1979/1986). *Laboratory Life: The Construction of Scientific Facts*. Princeton University Press.
**Key contribution:** Ethnographic study of the Salk Institute showing how scientific facts are socially constructed through inscription devices, literary strategies, and micro-negotiations. Pioneered the anthropological approach to science.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Demonstrates that what "looks like" a fact is the product of community practices and shared literary/rhetorical conventions — i.e., shared taste for what constitutes evidence.

### 9. Knorr-Cetina, K. (2017). "The Fabrication of Facts: Toward a Microsociology of Scientific Knowledge." In *Society and Knowledge*, Routledge.
**Key contribution:** Argued scientific knowledge production is a fabrication process involving selections shaped by social context rather than purely logical inference.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Reinforces that "taste" in research is not merely aesthetic but deeply embedded in the social process of fact-making.

## Bourdieu and the Scientific Field

### 10. Bourdieu, P. (1975). "The Specificity of the Scientific Field and the Social Conditions of the Progress of Reason." *Social Science Information*, 14(6), 19–47.
**Key contribution:** Introduced the concept of the scientific field as a space of competition where agents struggle for scientific capital (authority, recognition). Scientific practice is shaped by field position and habitus.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Taste in research = habitus — the internalized dispositions acquired through socialization in a field. What seems "interesting" or "elegant" reflects one's position and trajectory in the field.

### 11. Bourdieu, P. (1984). *Homo Academicus*. Éditions de Minuit / Stanford University Press (1988 English ed.).
**Key contribution:** Applied field theory to French academia, mapping the distribution of different forms of capital (academic vs. intellectual) and showing how the academic habitus reproduces itself.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Explains how academic communities reproduce shared taste through institutional mechanisms (hiring, publishing, prizes) that select for particular dispositions.

### 12. Bourdieu, P. (1984/2010). *Distinction: A Social Critique of the Judgement of Taste*. Routledge.
**Key contribution:** Demonstrated that aesthetic taste is socially structured — class position shapes cultural preferences through habitus. Taste functions as a marker of distinction and social reproduction.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Though focused on cultural consumption, provides the general theory of how taste is socially produced and reproduced that underpins analysis of scientific taste.

### 13. Bourdieu, P. (2004). *Science of Science and Reflexivity*. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Bourdieu's mature sociology of science, applying reflexive analysis to the scientific field itself. Argues the scientific field has specific autonomy but is still structured by power relations.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Most direct application of Bourdieu's framework to understanding how scientific communities develop and enforce shared standards of taste.

### 14. Lenoir, R. (2006). "Scientific Habitus: Pierre Bourdieu and the Collective Intellectual." *Theory, Culture & Society*, 23(6), 25–43.
**Key contribution:** Examines how Bourdieu conceived the scientific habitus as collectively formed through research center practices — science as a collective enterprise producing shared dispositions.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Directly addresses how research groups develop collective taste through shared practice.

### 15. Jeon, J. (2019). "Rethinking Scientific Habitus: Toward a Theory of Embodiment, Institutions, and Stratification of Science." *Engaging Science, Technology, and Society*, 5, 160–172.
**Key contribution:** Develops Bourdieu's scientific habitus concept, arguing it should account for embodiment, institutional structures, and stratification in how scientists acquire dispositions.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Updates habitus theory for understanding how taste is physically and institutionally embodied in research practice.

### 16. Hess, D.J. (2011). "Bourdieu and Science Studies: Toward a Reflexive Sociology." *Minerva*, 49(3), 283–303.
**Key contribution:** Bridges Bourdieu's sociology with STS (science and technology studies), analyzing how the habitus of STS scholars is shaped by their disciplinary position.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Meta-level reflection on how studying scientific taste is itself shaped by disciplinary taste.

### 17. Simon, R.M. (2011). "Habitus and Utopia in Science: Bourdieu, Mannheim, and the Role of Specialties in the Scientific Field." *Studies in Sociology of Science*, 2(2), 29–42.
**Key contribution:** Synthesizes Bourdieu's habitus with Mannheim's sociology of knowledge, examining how specialties within fields develop their own habitus that reproduces the field's structure.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Shows how sub-communities within a field develop distinct micro-tastes.

## Tacit Knowledge and Expertise

### 18. Collins, H.M. (2001). "Tacit Knowledge, Trust and the Q of Sapphire." *Social Studies of Science*, 31(1), 71–85.
**Key contribution:** Showed that tacit knowledge transfer in science requires prolonged social contact and trust — knowledge that "can be passed between scientists by personal contact but cannot be or has not been set out or published in full."
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Taste is a form of tacit knowledge — it can only be acquired through immersion in a community, not from reading papers alone.

### 19. Collins, H.M. (2010). *Tacit and Explicit Knowledge*. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Distinguished three types of tacit knowledge: relational (could be made explicit), somatic (embodied in the body), and collective (embedded in society). Collective tacit knowledge is the most sociologically significant.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Research taste = collective tacit knowledge — the unarticulated shared understanding of what's good, interesting, or promising.

### 20. Collins, H.M. & Evans, R. (2007). *Rethinking Expertise*. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Proposed a periodic table of expertise distinguishing contributory expertise (ability to contribute to a field) from interactional expertise (ability to talk the talk without doing the doing).
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Relevant to how AI systems might develop interactional expertise in research taste without contributory expertise.

### 21. Collins, H.M. (1985). *Changing Order: Replication and Induction in Scientific Practice*. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Showed through case studies (TEA laser, gravitational waves) that replicating experiments requires tacit knowledge transfer — formal instructions are never sufficient.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Demonstrates that the "style" of doing science includes unarticulated craft knowledge that shapes what communities find convincing.

### 22. Polanyi, M. (1966). *The Tacit Dimension*. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Foundational work on tacit knowledge: "we can know more than we can tell." Distinguished focal awareness from subsidiary awareness in skilled performance.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** The philosophical foundation for understanding research taste as a form of knowing that resists full articulation.

## Invisible Colleges and Scientific Communication

### 23. Crane, D. (1972). *Invisible Colleges: Diffusion of Knowledge in Scientific Communities*. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Showed that scientific communities form "invisible colleges" — informal networks of researchers who share knowledge, set agendas, and determine what's important. These networks follow logistic growth curves. Cited 5,000+.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Invisible colleges are the social infrastructure through which shared taste propagates. They determine what problems and approaches are fashionable.

### 24. Price, D.J. de Solla (1963). *Little Science, Big Science*. Columbia University Press.
**Key contribution:** Quantitative analysis of scientific growth showing exponential publication growth and the formation of elite networks. Coined the concept of the invisible college in modern science.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Foundational work on the social structure of science — taste is shaped by network position and the concentration of influence among elite researchers.

### 25. Lievrouw, L.A. (1989). "The Invisible College Reconsidered: Bibliometrics and the Development of Scientific Communication Theory." *Communication Research*, 16(5), 615–628.
**Key contribution:** Critically reviewed the invisible college concept using bibliometric methods, showing how informal communication networks shape scientific consensus.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Connects invisible colleges to communication patterns that transmit and reinforce shared taste.

### 26. Zuccala, A. (2006). "Modeling the Invisible College." *Journal of the American Society for Information Science and Technology*, 57(2), 152–168.
**Key contribution:** Updated the invisible college model for the electronic age, showing how digital communication creates new patterns of informal scientific networking. Cited 379.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Relevant to understanding how AI tools might reshape the invisible colleges that currently transmit research taste.

## Trading Zones and Boundary Objects

### 27. Galison, P. (1997). *Image and Logic: A Material Culture of Microphysics*. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Introduced "trading zones" — spaces where different scientific subcultures (theorists, experimentalists, instrument makers) coordinate despite incommensurable paradigms, developing pidgin/creole languages.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Trading zones show how different taste communities negotiate and partially merge their standards at disciplinary boundaries.

### 28. Galison, P. (2010). "Trading with the Enemy." In Collins, H., Evans, R., & Gorman, M. (Eds.), *Trading Zones and Interactional Expertise*, pp. 25–52. MIT Press.
**Key contribution:** Refined the trading zone concept, distinguishing different types (enforced vs. voluntary, homogeneous vs. heterogeneous) and their dynamics.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Provides taxonomy for understanding how taste hybridizes at interdisciplinary boundaries.

### 29. Star, S.L. & Griesemer, J.R. (1989). "Institutional Ecology, 'Translations' and Boundary Objects: Amateurs and Professionals in Berkeley's Museum of Vertebrate Zoology, 1907–39." *Social Studies of Science*, 19(3), 387–420.
**Key contribution:** Introduced "boundary objects" — objects plastic enough to adapt to different communities' needs yet robust enough to maintain identity across them (maps, specimens, standardized forms). Cited 14,000+.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Boundary objects enable coordination between communities with different tastes. Research Taster could function as a boundary object between AI and human research communities.

### 30. Trompette, P. & Vinck, D. (2009). "Revisiting the Notion of Boundary Object." *Revue d'Anthropologie des Connaissances*, 3(1), 3–25.
**Key contribution:** Critical review and extension of the boundary object concept 20 years after Star & Griesemer, examining its applications and limitations across diverse collaborative settings.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Updated framework for understanding how shared objects mediate between different epistemic cultures.

## Norms, Ethos, and Social Structure of Science

### 31. Merton, R.K. (1942/1973). "The Normative Structure of Science." In *The Sociology of Science*, pp. 267–278. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Articulated the four norms of the scientific ethos: communism (shared ownership of knowledge), universalism, disinterestedness, and organized skepticism (CUDOS). Foundational for sociology of science.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Mertonian norms define the idealized taste of science — what the community officially values. Real taste deviates from these norms in systematic ways (the "Matthew effect," stratification).

### 32. Merton, R.K. (1968). "The Matthew Effect in Science." *Science*, 159(3810), 56–63.
**Key contribution:** Showed that eminent scientists receive disproportionate credit for equivalent work — "the rich get richer." Demonstrates how social position shapes recognition.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Recognition structures reinforce taste hierarchies — what's "interesting" is partly a function of who says it.

### 33. Merton, R.K. (1938). "Science and the Social Order." *Philosophy of Science*, 5(3), 321–337.
**Key contribution:** Early analysis of the relationship between scientific norms and broader social structure, arguing science requires certain social conditions to flourish.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Shows how external social forces shape the internal taste of scientific communities.

### 34. Stehr, N. (2018). "The Ethos of Science Revisited: Social and Cognitive Norms." In *Nico Stehr: Pioneer in the Theory of Society and Knowledge*, pp. 197–215. Springer.
**Key contribution:** Revisited Merton's ethos framework, arguing for both social and cognitive norms in scientific practice and their evolution over time.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Updated perspective on how community norms (including taste) evolve.

## Disciplinary Cultures and Research Styles

### 35. Becher, T. & Trowler, P. (2001). *Academic Tribes and Territories: Intellectual Enquiry and the Culture of Disciplines* (2nd ed.). Open University Press.
**Key contribution:** Systematic analysis of how different academic disciplines constitute distinct "tribes" with characteristic cultures, identities, and modes of inquiry. Distinguished hard/soft, pure/applied dimensions.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Directly maps the landscape of disciplinary taste — different tribes have different aesthetics for what constitutes good research.

### 36. Becher, T. (1994). "The Significance of Disciplinary Differences." *Studies in Higher Education*, 19(2), 151–161.
**Key contribution:** Examined how disciplinary cultures operate at macro, meso, and micro levels, affecting everything from career patterns to communication styles.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Shows taste operates at multiple scales — from field-level paradigms to lab-level customs.

### 37. Becher, T. (1987). "The Disciplinary Shaping of the Profession." *Studies in Higher Education*, 12(3), 271–282.
**Key contribution:** Analyzed how disciplinary cultures shape academic professional identity, including what constitutes valued work and recognized expertise.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Demonstrates that taste is professionally constitutive — it defines who belongs.

### 38. Becher, T. (1987). "Disciplinary Discourse." *Studies in Higher Education*, 12(3), 261–274.
**Key contribution:** Showed how language use varies systematically across disciplines, reflecting deeper differences in knowledge structures and epistemic values.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Discourse style is a tangible manifestation of research taste — how you write signals what community you belong to.

## Additional Key Works

### 39. Hagstrom, W.O. (1965). *The Scientific Community*. Basic Books.
**Key contribution:** Analyzed science as a social system based on gift exchange — scientists give knowledge and receive recognition. This exchange system creates community norms.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** The gift economy of science structures what's valued (and thus what tastes develop) through recognition dynamics.

### 40. Whitley, R. (2000). *The Intellectual and Social Organization of the Sciences* (2nd ed.). Oxford University Press.
**Key contribution:** Developed a framework for comparing sciences based on two dimensions: mutual dependence (how much researchers depend on each other's work) and task uncertainty (how unpredictable research outcomes are).
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Provides structural variables that predict different taste regimes — high mutual dependence + low task uncertainty = strong shared taste.

### 41. Pickering, A. (1992). *Science as Practice and Culture*. University of Chicago Press.
**Key contribution:** Collected essays arguing for understanding science through its practices rather than its theories — the "practice turn" in science studies.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Taste is embedded in practice, not just belief. Understanding research taste requires understanding what scientists *do*, not just what they say.

### 42. Schirone, M. (2023). "Field, Capital, and Habitus: The Impact of Pierre Bourdieu on Bibliometrics." *Quantitative Science Studies*, 4(1), 186–207.
**Key contribution:** Bibliometric analysis of Bourdieu's influence on scientometrics, showing how field/capital/habitus concepts have been adopted to study scientific communities quantitatively.
**Relevance to Research Taster:** Bridges qualitative sociology of taste with quantitative approaches — relevant to operationalizing taste detection.

## Summary

This literature establishes that research communities develop shared taste and style through:

1. **Thought styles** (Fleck) and **paradigms** (Kuhn) — cognitive frameworks that filter what's interesting
2. **Epistemic cultures** (Knorr-Cetina) — deep structures of knowledge production practice
3. **Habitus** (Bourdieu) — internalized dispositions reproduced through field dynamics
4. **Tacit knowledge** (Collins, Polanyi) — unarticulated know-how transmitted through socialization
5. **Invisible colleges** (Crane, Price) — informal networks that propagate standards
6. **Trading zones** (Galison) and **boundary objects** (Star & Griesemer) — mechanisms for cross-community coordination
7. **Norms** (Merton) and **disciplinary cultures** (Becher) — institutional structures encoding values

For Research Taster, the key insight is that research taste is **socially constituted, tacitly transmitted, and community-specific**. Any AI system attempting to model or replicate it must grapple with these deeply social dimensions.
